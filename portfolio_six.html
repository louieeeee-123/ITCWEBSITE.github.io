Slide 3

Introduction

INTRODUCTION

Computers have evolved from room-sized machines to powerful devices that fit in our pockets, each designed for specific tasks and user needs. This variety in computing power, size, and cost means that a machine perfect for scientific research would be inefficient and wasteful for writing a school report.

This report provides a comparative study of the main categories of computers in use today: supercomputers, mainframes, minicomputers, servers, workstations, and microcomputers. By examining their specifications, working principles, and energy consumption, we can understand their distinct roles in our technological ecosystem.

The purpose of this study is to clearly differentiate these computer types and identify the specific fields where each excels.

Slide 6

Discussion

DISCUSSION

The comparison shows a clear hierarchy among computer types, balancing processing power, user capacity, and energy efficiency. At the top, supercomputers like Frontier use massive parallel processing for scientific breakthroughs but consume enormous energy. Mainframes like IBM z16 prioritize reliability and handling thousands of simultaneous transactions for critical industries like banking.

Servers act as network workhorses, serving multiple clients with shared resources, while minicomputers provide centralized power for medium-sized organizations. Workstations offer high-end performance for specialized single-user tasks like engineering, and microcomputers serve general-purpose needs with maximum efficiency for individual users.

Each computer type occupies a specific niche, with capabilities precisely matched to its intended use—from national research to personal computing.
