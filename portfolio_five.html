Introduction

INTRODUCTION

A motherboard is the main circuit board in a computer. It holds and connects all the important parts, like the CPU, memory, and storage, allowing them to work together. Motherboards come in different standard sizes, known as form factors.

The form factor decides the size of the computer case, how many components can be added, and the best use for the computer, from small home theaters to large workstations. This study compares the most common motherboard sizes.

Slide 5

Discussion

DISCUSSION

The evolution of motherboard form factors has been driven by the competing demands of performance, expandability, size reduction, thermal efficiency, and cost.

The dominant standard for decades has been the ATX family and its derivatives, which successfully balanced these factors for the mainstream desktop market. The AT form factor, its predecessor, was plagued by poor layout and usability.

While BTX was a technically superior design focused on better cooling, it failed to displace the entrenched ATX ecosystem. In the modern era, the rise of Small Form Factor (SFF) PCs has popularized Micro-ATX and Mini-ITX, which sacrifice some expansion to achieve a much smaller physical footprint.
