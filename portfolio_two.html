Data vs Information

DATA VS INFORMATION

Data

Refers to raw, unorganized facts and figures that lack context. It can be quantitative (numerical) or qualitative (descriptive). For example, the numbers "27," "150," and "2023" are data points.

Information

Is data that has been processed, organized, structured, or presented in a given context to make it meaningful and useful. It answers questions like "who," "what," "where," and "when." Using the data above, information would be: "The average temperature in July (27°C) was 150% higher than the previous year (2022)."

Information Systems

INFORMATION SYSTEMS

An Information System (IS) is an integrated set of components for collecting, storing, processing, and disseminating information. It includes hardware, software, data, procedures, and people. The primary purpose of an IS is to support decision-making, coordination, control, analysis, and visualization in an organization. Information systems transform raw data into useful information through a series of activities: input, processing, output, and feedback. They are not just technological artifacts but also encompass the organizational and human dimensions that interact with the technology.

Types of Support Systems

DIFFERENT TYPES OF SUPPORT SYSTEMS IN INFORMATION SYSTEMS

Slide 6

Transaction Processing & Management Information Systems

TRANSACTION PROCESSING SYSTEMS (TPS)

Process routine business transactions (e.g., sales orders, payroll, reservations). They are the foundation for other systems, providing the raw operational data.

MANAGEMENT INFORMATION SYSTEMS (MIS)

Use the data from TPS to produce summarized, structured reports (e.g., monthly sales reports, budget summaries) for middle management.

Slide 7

Decision Support & Executive Information Systems

DECISION SUPPORT SYSTEMS (DSS)

Interactive systems that help managers use data and models to solve semi-structured problems. They focus on analytical capabilities and "what-if" analysis.

EXECUTIVE INFORMATION SYSTEMS (EIS):

Tailored for senior executives, providing a broad, aggregated view of organizational performance through graphs and dashboards, often with drill-down capabilities. They address strategic, unstructured decisions.

Slide 8

Expert Systems

EXPERT SYSTEMS (ES):

A type of knowledge-based system that captures human expertise in a specific domain to provide advice and solve problems (e.g., diagnostic systems, loan application advisors).

Analysis/Reaction Part 1

ANALYSIS/REACTION

Humans innovate and evolve with technology because we're always trying to solve a problem or make life easier. It starts with a simple need, we're tired of doing boring, repetitive tasks. So, we create systems like Transaction Processing Systems (TPS) to handle that work for us like a robot that does all the data entry. This frees us up to think about bigger things, it's not about being lazy it's about thinking efficiently.

But then, we face a new problem: all that data is messy and hard to understand. So, we innovate again. We build Management Information Systems (MIS) to turn numbers into clear reports like turning a pile of scattered notes into a simple summary. Now we can see what's actually happening in reports.

Analysis/Reaction Part 3

ANALYSIS/REACTION

Still, we want more. We don't just want to understand the present, we want to predict the future. So, we develop Decision Support Systems (DSS). These let us play out what–if scenarios safely, like testing decisions in a digital sandbox before making them in real life. To make better decisions through these what if scenarios, innovations are what makes this possible a simple what if.

And we don't stop there. We ask: Can we capture human expertise itself? That curiosity leads to Expert Systems—tools that try to think like a seasoned expert, making knowledge reusable and lasting beyond any one person.

Slide 12

Analysis/Reaction Part 4

ANALYSIS/REACTION

At every step, it's not really about the machines. It's about our frustration, our curiosity, our desire to do more and know more. We're always looking at what we've built and thinking: How can we make this better? How can we go further? What could we do to make this work? That restless drive to improve, to work smarter, see clearer, and reach further is what keeps pushing innovation forward. We aren't just building systems; we're expanding what humans can do.
